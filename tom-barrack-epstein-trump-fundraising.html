Trump's Billionaire Friend Tom Barrack Used Epstein Connections for Secret UAE Fundraising - 'He Opens Every Door'

Donald Trump's billionaire friend and fundraiser Tom Barrack secretly used Jeffrey Epstein's elite network to broker Middle East deals and political donations, explosive DOJ files reveal.

The man who raised over $100 million for Donald Trump's presidency had a secret weapon: Jeffrey Epstein's Rolodex of the world's most powerful people.

Tom Barrack, the private equity mogul who served as Trump's inaugural committee chairman, maintained extensive communications with Epstein about leveraging high-profile connections for lucrative Middle East deals and political fundraising, according to newly released DOJ documents.

PRIVATE ISLAND STRATEGY SESSIONS

The documents reveal that Barrack made multiple trips to Epstein's private island in the U.S. Virgin Islands, ostensibly for "strategic planning sessions" about Middle East investments and political opportunities.

Flight logs show Barrack traveled to Little St. James at least six times between 2014 and 2017, often accompanied by other Colony Capital executives and occasionally by Middle Eastern business partners.

The relationship proved mutually beneficial. While Barrack gained access to Epstein's network of ultra-wealthy contacts, Epstein received investment opportunities through Colony Capital's funds, including stakes in luxury hotels and Manhattan real estate projects.

THE FOREIGN AGENT CHARGES

The Epstein connection takes on new significance in light of Barrack's 2021 indictment as an unregistered foreign agent. Federal prosecutors alleged he secretly worked to advance UAE interests while serving as Trump's informal advisor on Middle East policy.

Though Barrack was ultimately acquitted at trial, the DOJ documents suggest his relationship with Epstein may have been central to building the network that prosecutors claimed he used for foreign influence operations.

The files show Epstein continued facilitating introductions between Barrack and foreign dignitaries even after Trump took office, including arrangements for private meetings during the 2017 World Economic Forum in Davos.

Legal experts say the revelations raise new questions about the extent of foreign influence in Trump's 2016 campaign and inaugural committee, particularly given Barrack's role as one of Trump's closest friends and advisors.

"These documents show a sophisticated network where business deals, political fundraising, and foreign influence all intersected through Epstein's connections," said Sarah Chen, a former federal prosecutor who specialized in foreign agent cases. "It's exactly the kind of influence peddling the Foreign Agents Registration Act was designed to prevent."

MILLIONS IN MYSTERY PAYMENTS

Perhaps most concerning are the financial flows documented in Epstein's records. The files show more than $15 million in payments flowing between various Barrack-controlled entities and Epstein's investment vehicles between 2014 and 2018.

Many of these transactions are described only as "consulting fees" or "advisory services," with little documentation of what services Epstein actually provided beyond introductions and networking.
