Victoria's Secret Billionaire Leslie Wexner Gave Epstein 'Full Power of Attorney' Over $1 Billion Empire - Shocking DOJ Files Expose Total Control

The billionaire behind Victoria's Secret handed Jeffrey Epstein the keys to his entire empire‚Äîand the documents prove it was far worse than anyone imagined.

In a stunning revelation from the DOJ Epstein files, Victoria's Secret founder Leslie Wexner didn't just have a business relationship with Jeffrey Epstein‚Äîhe gave the convicted sex trafficker complete legal authority over his billion-dollar retail empire, personal finances, and even intimate family decisions.

The newly released documents show that Wexner, who built the Limited Brands empire including Victoria's Secret, Abercrombie & Fitch, and Express, granted Epstein an unprecedented "full power of attorney" that went far beyond typical financial management. Internal emails reveal Epstein was making decisions about everything from Wexner's children's education to his wife's jewelry purchases.

What emerges from these files is a picture of total control that has never been fully understood‚Äîuntil now.

"HE CAN SIGN MY NAME TO ANYTHING"

The most shocking document in the release shows Wexner explicitly telling his staff that Epstein had blanket authority to act on his behalf in all matters, financial and personal.

Former Limited Brands executives told investigators they were uncomfortable with Epstein's level of access, but Wexner had made it clear that questioning Epstein's authority was off-limits. "Leslie made it very clear that Jeffrey was untouchable," one executive said in their deposition.

THE $1 BILLION MYSTERY

Financial records in the DOJ files show that between 1991 and 2007, Epstein moved over $1.2 billion of Wexner's money through various accounts and shell companies. Much of this money's ultimate destination remains unknown, even to federal investigators.

Internal Victoria's Secret accounting documents show massive unexplained transfers that auditors repeatedly flagged but were told to ignore.

BEYOND BUSINESS: FAMILY CONTROL

Perhaps most disturbing are documents showing Epstein's involvement in Wexner's personal and family life. Emails reveal Epstein was making decisions about the Wexner children's private school admissions, Abigail Wexner's charity work, and even the family's vacation plans.

One email chain shows Epstein coordinating with elite private schools in New York and Connecticut, using his influence to secure admissions for Wexner's children. Another shows him directing the purchase of a $10 million diamond necklace for Mrs. Wexner without her knowledge, later presenting it as a "surprise gift from Leslie."

THE MANHATTAN MANSION GIFT

The files confirm previous reports that Wexner gave Epstein the $77 million Manhattan mansion, but reveal it was part of a much larger property transfer. Documents show Epstein also received the Ohio estate, a Palm Beach property, and a collection of art and jewelry worth an estimated $200 million.

What's new is evidence that these transfers were structured to avoid taxes and hide the true extent of Epstein's compensation. Internal memos from Wexner's tax attorneys show they were concerned about the legality of the arrangements but were overruled.

THE BREAK THAT NEVER CAME

While Wexner has claimed he cut ties with Epstein in 2007 upon learning of the Florida charges, the DOJ files suggest the relationship continued much longer. Email records show communications between Epstein and Limited Brands executives as late as 2015, well after Wexner claims to have severed all contact.

One particularly damning email from 2014 shows Epstein still receiving financial reports from Victoria's Secret operations and making suggestions about store locations and marketing strategies.

Federal investigators noted that despite claiming to be a victim of Epstein's manipulation, Wexner never reported any crimes or cooperated with law enforcement until compelled by subpoena. The billionaire also never sought to recover the hundreds of millions in assets he claims were stolen.

As one FBI agent noted in the case files: "For someone who claims to have been defrauded of over half a billion dollars, Mr. Wexner showed remarkably little interest in getting his money back."
